# Retail Management Platform Investor Pitch And Financial Model

## 1. Executive Summary

This product is shaping into a retail-focused ERP platform for Indian SMBs. The backend is already aligned with core operational workflows and the web UI is well underway, while the mobile app is in progress. Based on the current codebase, the platform already covers:

- Sales: POS, quotes, invoices, orders, receipts, returns, recurring invoices
- Purchases: orders, receipts, supplier payments, returns, expenses
- Inventory: items, warehouses, stock adjustments, stock transfers, scan and track
- Banking: accounts, transactions, statement import, reconciliation
- Accounting and controls: chart of accounts, vouchers, roles, permissions, settings
- Business intelligence: dashboard summary, profitability, dues, stock alerts, GST reports

This positions the company as more than a billing app. The stronger pitch is:

`An operating system for independent retailers and growing multi-store businesses that unifies sales, inventory, purchasing, payments, banking visibility, and compliance in one workflow.`

## 2. Investor Pitch Narrative

### The problem

Small and mid-sized retailers often run their business across disconnected tools:

- Billing or POS software for sales
- Excel or notebooks for inventory
- WhatsApp for approvals and follow-ups
- Separate accounting or CA workflows for books and GST
- No clean real-time view of stock, dues, margins, or store-level performance

This creates stock leakage, poor replenishment, delayed collections, weak audit trails, and owner dependence for daily operations.

### The solution

Our platform gives retailers one system to run daily operations:

- Sell faster through POS and invoicing
- Control inventory across products, warehouses, and transfers
- Track purchase cycles and supplier obligations
- Monitor receivables, reconciliation, and business health
- Improve decision-making with profitability and operational reports
- Extend usage to the field with mobile workflows as the app is completed

### Why this matters

Retail software in this segment often solves one layer only. We are building a workflow stack that can become system-of-record software for the merchant. That improves retention, expansion revenue, and defensibility.

### Why now

- Indian SMB digitization is still uneven, especially beyond pure billing tools
- Retailers increasingly expect mobile-first operations and real-time reporting
- GST, compliance, and working capital pressure increase the need for structured records
- Multi-store and omni-channel retail operations need tighter inventory discipline

## 3A. Product Roadmap: Phase 1 To Phase 3

This roadmap is strongest when presented as compounding value, not as three disconnected product ideas.

### Phase 1: Retail Operating System

Phase 1 is the operational foundation already reflected in the current product direction.

Core value created:

- Sales workflows including POS, invoices, orders, receipts, and returns
- Purchase workflows including orders, receipts, supplier payments, returns, and expenses
- Inventory control through items, warehouses, stock adjustments, stock transfers, and scan flows
- Banking visibility, receivables tracking, profitability reporting, GST reporting, and accounting controls

Strategic value created:

- The platform becomes the store's system of record
- Product, order, inventory, supplier, and customer data become structured and reusable
- Merchants build daily operational dependency on the system

### Phase 2: Digital Storefront For Each Organization

In Phase 2, each organization gets its own digital storefront where customers can place orders online.

Core value created:

- Customers can browse and place online orders with a specific store
- Orders flow directly into the platform as operational orders
- The store can accept or reject incoming orders
- The platform can levy a small commission fee on each online order
- Customers can see real-time stock availability because stock is already controlled through purchase orders, goods receipt, and inventory updates in Phase 1

Why this matters:

- Merchants do not need a separate e-commerce system
- Existing inventory and order logic are reused instead of rebuilt
- The platform expands from internal operations software into a revenue-generating commerce channel

Investor framing:

`Phase 2 monetizes the operational system built in Phase 1 by converting structured catalog, pricing, and inventory data into a live digital ordering channel for each merchant.`

### Phase 3: Unified Marketplace Across The Platform

In Phase 3, the platform evolves from individual storefronts into a marketplace spanning multiple stores and shared customers.

Core value created:

- Customers can use the same account structure across the platform
- Existing customers can be enabled for marketplace access and set their passwords when they register
- Customers can discover products across all participating stores
- Customers can browse categories and use multiple filters to narrow down products
- The platform becomes a shared demand and discovery layer on top of merchant supply

Why this matters:

- The platform already has stores, products, customers, and order workflows
- Marketplace participation grows from an installed merchant base instead of cold-start supply
- Product discovery and cross-store demand create stronger network effects than standalone merchant software

Investor framing:

`Phase 3 transforms the product from merchant software into a multi-store commerce marketplace built on top of verified inventory, active merchants, and existing customer identity.`

### The strategic progression

The roadmap should be explained in one line like this:

`Phase 1 builds merchant workflow and trusted data, Phase 2 turns each merchant into a digital commerce node, and Phase 3 connects those nodes into a marketplace.`

This framing is important because it shows investors that later phases are a direct expansion of the workflow, data, and merchant adoption created in Phase 1.

## 4. Business Model And Pricing

Illustrative monthly pricing in INR:

The pricing strategy should start with an entry point that feels accessible to merchants already familiar with low-cost billing tools, then expand into a deeper operating and finance stack over time.

Illustrative monthly pricing in INR:

- Basic: `249 per month`, billed annually, for single-store billing, basic inventory, customer management, and core operational usage
- Growth: `1,999 per month`, billed annually, for stronger inventory controls, purchase workflows, business reports, dues, and internal controls
- Pro Finance: `6,999 per month`, billed annually, for finance workflows, reconciliation visibility, deeper reporting, and stronger control layers
- Multi-store: `14,999 per month`, billed annually, for branch control, warehouse workflows, role permissions, and advanced operations across locations

This lets the platform enter the market at a price point comparable to lightweight SMB software, while still creating a natural path toward a higher-value operating and finance platform.

### The upgrade philosophy

Even when a customer starts on a basic subscription, the platform can continue maintaining accounting and audit-ready data structures in the backend.

That creates three strategic advantages:

- The customer gets immediate value at a low entry price without being forced into a heavy workflow on day one
- The platform can demonstrate deeper financial insights over time using the same underlying data
- When the customer is ready to upgrade, the transition is lighter because the accounting foundation already exists in the system

This is an important investor point because it shows that entry-level pricing does not mean entry-level data value.

Recommended framing:

`We can acquire merchants at an accessible starting price, but we still structure accounting and audit-relevant data in the backend from day one. That allows us to prove more value over time and upsell without forcing disruptive migration later.`

### Billing and discount structure

The entry plan should remain simple and cash-flow friendly.

- Basic plan starts at `249 per month`, but is billed annually
- Customers who commit for `3 years` receive an additional long-term discount
- Customers who commit for `5 years` receive a deeper long-term discount
- Customers upgrading from an existing plan receive an additional upgrade discount because their data and workflow are already inside the platform

Recommended drafting:

- Annual billing: standard price
- `3-year` commitment: `10%` discount on the committed plan value
- `5-year` commitment: `20%` discount on the committed plan value
- Upgrade from an existing paid plan: additional `5%` upgrade benefit on the new committed plan value or equivalent prorated credit

This approach improves cash flow, rewards commitment, and lowers upgrade friction without collapsing the pricing structure.

### Promise protection for early stores

This is important operationally and reputationally.

- If a commitment was made during onboarding, the company should honor that commitment
- Existing stores should not feel locked in or pressured unfairly once their data is on the platform
- Founding merchants can be grandfathered into the promise originally made to them for a defined period or feature scope
- New pricing can be applied cleanly to new stores and to future upgrades, instead of retroactively breaking trust with existing ones

Recommended investor framing:

`We use low-friction annual pricing to begin monetization early, but we also protect trust by honoring onboarding commitments made to early merchants. That helps us build a credible reputation while creating a path to paid expansion and stronger prepaid cash flow.`

Optional add-ons:

- Setup and onboarding: `10,000` to `25,000` one-time
- Additional branch or warehouse package
- Mobile sales or field workflows
- Advanced analytics and automation

Commission-based expansion:

- Phase 2 storefront commission: `2%` on online orders placed through merchant storefronts
- Phase 3 marketplace commission: `5%` on marketplace orders, reflecting the additional platform overhead, discovery, customer management, and transaction support involved in marketplace operations

This should be positioned as a meaningful monetization layer on top of SaaS, while still remaining merchant-friendly.

### How to explain the pricing ladder to investors

- The low-end plan reduces friction in SMB acquisition
- The backend still captures structured accounting and audit-friendly data from the start
- That data becomes proof of value for reporting, control, reconciliation, compliance, and decision support
- Upgrades become easier because merchants do not need to re-implement their operations or migrate to a new system
- This improves expansion revenue potential while reducing upgrade pain
- Founding-customer pricing can begin monetization without breaking trust with stores that joined under earlier promises

## 8. Break-Even View

If blended monthly ARPA is `3,119` and steady-state gross margin is `75%`, then gross profit per customer per month is approximately:

`3,119 x 75% = 2,339 INR`

If fixed operating expense is roughly `225,000 INR` per month in the current lean setup, EBITDA break-even customer count is:

`225,000 / 2,339 = 96 customers`

A useful investor line is:

`In the current founder-led operating model, the business can approach EBITDA break-even at roughly 95 to 100 active customers at the modeled blended pricing level, with much larger upside as customers expand into higher-value plans, prepaid commitments, and transaction-linked revenue.`

For immediate survival-mode planning, a second line can be:

`Before full-scale subscription conversion, even a small founding-customer plan across early stores can help cover server and hosting costs without violating prior onboarding commitments.`

## 10. Talking Points For Investors

- We are not building a single-feature POS app; we are building a workflow system for store operations.
- The current product direction already covers revenue, inventory, purchasing, dues, reconciliation, and reporting.
- This creates higher retention potential than standalone billing software.
- Multi-store operations, role controls, and banking visibility create upsell paths.
- Mobile can expand usage from owner/admin usage to store-floor and field usage.
- Once embedded as system-of-record software, switching costs become meaningful.
- Phase 2 opens a merchant-specific digital sales channel using the same operational backbone.
- Phase 3 uses the installed base of merchants and customers to create a marketplace rather than starting from zero supply.
- Phase 2 and 3 add transaction-linked monetization on top of SaaS, which improves upside without depending only on subscription pricing.
- A `2%` storefront take rate and `5%` marketplace take rate can be framed as merchant-friendly while still expanding platform revenue.
- Entry pricing can stay accessible for SMB acquisition, while the backend still builds the accounting and audit data required for higher-tier finance workflows later.
- This makes plan upgrades less disruptive because the system is already collecting the deeper data foundation before the customer fully adopts advanced finance features.
- Early monetization can begin through a low-entry annual plan designed to cover infrastructure costs while preserving upgrade headroom.
- Trust matters because merchant data sits on the platform, so previously committed onboarding promises should be honored and new pricing should be introduced transparently.
- Prepaid annual, 3-year, and 5-year commitments can improve cash flow without changing the underlying product strategy.
